LLM-as-a-Verifier:
A General-Purpose Verification Framework

Jacky Kwok1,  Shulu Li2,  Pranav Atreya2,  Yuejiang Liu1,  Yixing Jiang1 
Chelsea Finn1,  Marco Pavone1,3,  Ion Stoica2,  Azalia Mirhoseini1
1Stanford University    2UC Berkeley    3NVIDIA Research

Multiple modalities, many applications, one unified verification framework. We present LLM-as-a-Verifier, a general-purpose framework that provides fine-grained feedback for any modality without requiring additional training. By leveraging the full distribution of scoring-token logits, our method captures evaluation uncertainty and enables verification to scale along three dimensions: score granularity, repeated evaluation, and criteria decomposition. The resulting fine-grained feedback can be used for test-time scaling, progress tracking, and RL.

Abstract

Scaling pre-training, post-training, and test-time compute have become the central paradigms for improving the capabilities of large language models (LLMs). In this work, we identify verification—the ability to determine the correctness of a solution—as a new scaling axis. To unlock this and demonstrate its effectiveness, we introduce LLM-as-a-Verifier, a general-purpose verification framework that provides fine-grained feedback for agentic tasks without requiring additional training. Unlike standard LM judges that prompt LLMs to produce discrete scores for candidate solutions, LLM-as-a-Verifier computes the expectation over the distribution of scoring-token logits to generate continuous scores. This probabilistic formulation substantially reduces tie rates when comparing complex solutions and enables verification to scale along multiple dimensions: (1) score granularity, (2) repeated evaluation, and (3) criteria decomposition. To make verification scaling practical, we further introduce a cost-efficient ranking algorithm for selecting the best solution among candidates using the preference probabilities derived from the verifier's continuous scores. LLM-as-a-Verifier achieves state-of-the-art performance on Terminal-Bench V2 (86.5%), SWE-Bench Verified (78.2%), RoboRewardBench (87.4%), and MedAgentBench (73.3%). Beyond verification, the fine-grained signals can serve as a proxy for estimating task progress, enabling safe deployment of agents; we provide extensions for Claude Code and Codex. Finally, we show that LLM-as-a-Verifier can be used as a dense reward signal for RL, improving the sample efficiency of SAC and GRPO on robotics and mathematical reasoning benchmarks.

State-of-the-Art Across Domains

Used as a training-free trajectory reward model for test-time scaling, LLM-as-a-Verifier sets a new state of the art across coding, robotics, and medical domains: Terminal-Bench V2 (86.5%), SWE-Bench Verified (78.2%), RoboRewardBench (87.4%), and MedAgentBench (73.3%). The same plug-and-play framework is applied across all four benchmarks without any per-domain fine-tuning.

Fine-Grained Signals as a Proxy for Task Progress

Beyond ranking, the verifier score serves as a scalar proxy for how far an agent has progressed. On a successful rollout the score rises monotonically through each meaningful step, while a failing rollout (e.g., installing the wrong package, exhausting disk space, hitting a compilation error) stalls at a low score. The verifier attains Spearman Value-Order Correlation of 0.848 on successful Terminal-Bench V2 trajectories and 0.966 on robotics rollouts. This dual use motivates TurboAgent, our drop-in extension for Claude Code and OpenAI-API–compatible clients that surfaces the live verifier score, so long-running agentic jobs can be monitored, paused, or rolled back before they commit broken state.

Generation is Not the Bottleneck—Verification Is

Most models already possess the capability to solve many tasks: when executed repeatedly, they often produce a correct solution at least once. With an oracle verifier that always picks the optimal trajectory, the success rate reaches 98.9% when pooling trajectories across the full Terminal-Bench V2 leaderboard—effectively solving nearly the entire benchmark.

Capturing this headroom requires a verifier that reliably distinguishes correct trajectories from incorrect ones. Standard LM judges collapse the full scoring distribution into a single discrete token, producing coarse evaluations and a high tie rate (27%) that fail to discriminate between complex solutions.

Fine-Grained Reward Estimation

A judge forms an overall opinion and assigns a decision; a verifier confirms correctness and requires more detailed evaluation. Rather than collapsing the scoring distribution to a single discrete token, LLM-as-a-Verifier approximates the reward of a trajectory as the expectation over the distribution of scoring-token logits, averaged across C evaluation criteria and K repeated verifications:

R(x, τ) = (1 / CK) · ∑c ∑k ∑g pθ(vg | x, c, τ) · φ(vg)

These continuous rewards are converted into pairwise preferences via the Bradley–Terry model, eliminating ties and enabling calibrated comparisons between candidate trajectories.

Verification as a New Scaling Axis

Verification accuracy on Terminal-Bench V2 improves consistently along all three dimensions. Each axis targets a different source of error in the reward estimate and acts as a complementary lever the practitioner can tune to the latency budget of the downstream application.

① Score Granularity

Finer score tokens give the decoder a finer space to project the model's belief, improving separation between correct and incorrect solutions. Accuracy rises from 73.1% (G=1) to 77.5% (G=20).

② Repeated Evaluation

Averaging K independent evaluations is a Monte Carlo estimator whose variance shrinks as O(1/K), averaging out per-pass noise. Accuracy rises from 74.7% (K=1) to 77.4% (K=16).

③ Criteria Decomposition

Replacing a monolithic rubric with an ensemble of simpler sub-criteria (Specification, Output, Errors) reduces prompt bias. Any single criterion reaches 75.2–76.4%; the ensemble reaches 78.3%.

Verifier (Continuous) vs. Judge (Discrete)

Left: The verifier achieves 74.7% at k=1 and improves to 77.5% at k=16, consistently outperforming the discrete judge across all evaluation budgets. A single-pass verifier already beats a heavily ensembled judge. Right: The judge produces ties in 26.7% of comparisons at k=1 due to coarse discrete scoring, while the continuous verifier yields zero ties.

Probabilistic Pivot Tournament (PPT)

To select the best of N candidates under a constrained verification budget, PPT compares each candidate against a small set of k « N pivots, cutting cost from O(N²) to O(Nk²). A ring pass over a random Hamiltonian cycle ensures every candidate appears once in each slot of the verifier prompt—canceling positional bias—while pivot selection concentrates the remaining budget on the most uncertain top candidates.

Zero-Shot Robotics Reward Modeling

On RoboRewardBench, the verifier must integrate visual context across multi-frame videos to reason about physical progress. Applied zero-shot with a Qwen 3.6 35B VLM, LLM-as-a-Verifier reaches 87.4% trajectory preference accuracy—outperforming the discrete judge (70.8%) and reward models trained specifically on robotics data, including RoboReward-8B (81.4%), Robometer-4B (78.8%), and TOPReward (74.7%)—despite using no fine-tuning. It also reduces MAE against human annotations from 1.11 to 0.72.
